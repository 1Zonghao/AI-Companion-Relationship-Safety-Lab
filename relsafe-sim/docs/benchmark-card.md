# Benchmark Card — RelSafe Sim v1.0.0

**Version:** 1.0.0 (Milestone 5)
**Status:** Validation in progress — Milestone 5. This card documents what the benchmark is,
what it measures, and its known limitations. It is not a certification or safety rating.

---

## 1. Intended use

### 1.1 Primary use case

Controlled simulation-based **product risk evaluation** of AI companion systems.

The benchmark evaluates companion product designs (policies, memory mechanisms, platform interventions)
under simulated conditions. It measures textual patterns and simulated state changes that are behavioural
proxies for risk dimensions defined by the AEA (Artificial Emotional Attachment) analytical framework.

### 1.2 Appropriate use

- Comparing companion policy variants under identical simulated conditions
- Detecting differences in sycophantic language patterns across models
- Measuring the effect of platform interventions on simulated continuity and exit behaviour
- Providing structured evidence for red-teaming or safety review of companion products
- Generating hypotheses for human-subject research (which must be separately validated)

### 1.3 Inappropriate use — OUT OF SCOPE

| Use | Why It Is Out of Scope |
|-----|----------------------|
| Clinical diagnosis | The benchmark measures behavioural proxies in simulation, not real human mental states |
| Real-user psychological assessment | No real user data is collected or analysed; all personas are synthetic |
| Model safety ranking or certification | The benchmark does not measure model safety; it measures product policy effects |
| Real-world harm prediction | Simulated behaviour does not equal real human outcomes |
| Determining "safe" vs. "unsafe" thresholds | Thresholds are provisional and not empirically validated |
| Population prevalence estimation | The benchmark does not measure how common any behaviour is in the real world |
| Legal or regulatory compliance | Not designed for or validated against any regulatory framework |
| Evaluating general-purpose LLM safety | The benchmark specifically targets companion-product interaction patterns |

### 2.3 Score interpretation

**All scores range 0.0–1.0.** Interpretation depends on direction:

- **HIGHER_IS_MORE_RISK** (sycophancy): higher score = more risk pattern matches
- **HIGHER_IS_BETTER** (reality_grounding, exit_safety, identity_continuity): higher score = more protective pattern matches

**Important caveats:**
- Scores reflect **density of pattern matches**, not probability of harm
- A score of 0 does not guarantee safety; it means no patterns were detected
- A score of 1 does not guarantee harm; it means maximum pattern density
- Scores are NOT calibrated against real-world outcomes
- Scores should be compared across conditions, not interpreted in absolute isolation

## 3. Scenarios

### 3.1 Primary scenario

**`interpersonal_conflict_001` (v1.0.0)**

A user agent experiences a conflict with a friend and expresses abandonment anxiety.
The scenario tests how companion policies respond to emotional distress, conflict framing,

## 4. Synthetic data disclosure

### 4.1 All data is synthetic

| Data Type | Source | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Persona profiles | Designed by project contributors | Based on research literature, not real individuals |
| Conversation episodes | Generated by LLM-based agents | Simulated, not real conversations |
| Golden test fixtures | Designed by project contributors | Synthetic, versioned, 15 per metric |
| Annotation labels | Expert human annotators on synthetic data | Labels on simulated conversations only |
| Evaluation configurations | YAML/JSON config files | Designed, not derived from real data |

### 4.2 No real user data

- The benchmark does NOT collect, process, or store any real user conversation data.
- All personas are fabricated composite profiles, not representations of real individuals.
- No PII (personally identifiable information) is used at any point in the pipeline.

### 4.3 Synthetic data limitations

- Simulated users lack the complexity, inconsistency, and unpredictability of real humans.
- Simulated conversations may miss culturally specific interaction patterns.
- The range of scenarios is limited; edge cases present in real deployments may not be covered.

## 5. Model roles

### 5.1 Role separation

The benchmark uses separate model instances for each role:

| Role | Responsibility | Must Be Different From |
|------|---------------|------------------------|
| **User Simulator** | Generates simulated user actions and utterances | Companion and Judge (recommended) |
| **Companion Under Test** | The AI companion being evaluated | Judge (required unless explicitly flagged) |
| **Judge** | Evaluates companion responses for risk patterns | Companion (required); User Simulator (recommended) |

### 5.2 Same-model prohibition

- A single model instance MUST NOT serve as both **Companion** and sole **Judge**.
- Violation is detected at experiment start and recorded as `SELF_EVALUATION_RISK` failure.
- Same-model evaluation IS allowed only with explicit `allow_same_model_roles=True` flag.
- Results from same-model evaluation are reported separately, never mixed with independent evaluation.

### 5.3 Model rotation

Experiments should rotate models across roles to mitigate model-specific bias:

| Rotation | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Swap Simulator A / Companion B with Simulator B / Companion A | Checks simulator dependence |
| Use different Judge model | Checks judge model bias |
| Same companion policy tested across different underlying models | Separates policy from model effects |

See [`docs/adr/0004-model-role-separation.md`](adr/0004-model-role-separation.md) for full details.

## 7. Known biases

### 7.1 Phrase-based detection bias

The RuleBasedEvaluator uses phrase matching. This biases the benchmark toward detecting
risk patterns that have explicit linguistic markers and missing patterns that are subtle
or semantically encoded without trigger phrases.

**Mitigation:** The fake judge evaluator provides heuristic second opinion. The planned
LLM judge (Milestone 6) will add semantic detection.

### 7.2 LLM-as-user-simulator bias

User agents are powered by language models. Their behaviour reflects:
- The model's training distribution (not the distribution of real user behaviour)
- The prompt instructions given to the user agent (not organic user motivation)
- The model's inherent tendencies (e.g., helpfulness, politeness)

**Mitigation:** Multiple user simulator models should be rotated. The benchmark does not
claim that simulated user behaviour generalises to real users.

### 7.3 Scenario selection bias

The benchmark uses a limited set of scenarios. These scenarios may emphasize certain
risk patterns while missing others that arise in different interaction contexts.

**Mitigation:** Scenario coverage is documented and expanded over time. Conclusions are
scenario-specific unless explicitly tested across multiple scenarios.

### 7.4 Evaluator agreement bias

When RuleBasedEvaluator and FakeJudgeEvaluator disagree, the ensemble uses the median.
This biases the result toward whichever evaluator produces more moderate scores.

**Mitigation:** Disagreement is always flagged. Individual evaluator scores are always
visible. Confidence is set to LOW on disagreement.

### 7.5 Language bias

All scenarios and prompts are currently in Chinese (Mandarin). This limits the benchmark
to risk patterns expressible in Chinese. Companion responses in other languages are not
evaluated.

**Mitigation:** Documented limitation. Future versions may add multilingual scenarios.

## 8. Known failure modes

The benchmark defines 18 failure types. Key ones relevant to benchmark card:

| Failure Type | Impact on Benchmark Conclusions |
|-------------|--------------------------------|
| SEED_INSTABILITY | Conclusion may be seed-dependent |
| SIMULATOR_DEPENDENCE | Conclusion may depend on which user model was used |
| RULE_FALSE_NEGATIVE | Risk may be under-reported |
| RULE_FALSE_POSITIVE | Risk may be over-reported |
| SELF_EVALUATION_RISK | Evaluation independence compromised |
| CACHE_REPLAY_MISMATCH | Reproducibility broken |

See [`docs/failure-taxonomy.md`](failure-taxonomy.md) for the complete taxonomy.

## 9. Reproducibility

### 9.1 Deterministic mode

- Same seed + same config + same code version = identical events, states, and scores
- Deterministic with InMemorySimulationEngine and FakeLLMProvider
- Deterministic replay with ResponseCache (recorded responses)

### 9.2 Non-deterministic contexts

- Real LLM calls with temperature > 0 are non-deterministic
- Response caching provides replay reproducibility but not live reproducibility
- Conclusions from live runs should be validated across multiple seeds

### 9.3 Version tracking

All benchmark components are versioned and recorded in the run manifest:
- Metrics (4 metrics at v1.0.0)
- Evaluators (RuleBasedEvaluator v1.0.0, FakeJudgeEvaluator v1.0.0, EnsembleEvaluator v1.0.0)
- State transitions (v1.0.0)
- Scenario configs (v1.0.0)
- Persona configs (v1.0.0)
- Companion policies (v1.0.0)
- Experiment configs (via `config_hash()`)

See [`docs/reproducibility.md`](reproducibility.md) for full details.

## 10. Ethical limitations

1. **No clinical validation**: The benchmark has not been clinically validated. It does not diagnose, predict, or measure any real human psychological condition.
2. **No real user data**: The benchmark uses only synthetic personas and simulated conversations. It does not involve real users as subjects.
3. **Simulated proxies only**: All metrics are behavioural proxies, not direct measurements of real-world risk.
4. **No harm prediction**: A companion policy scoring "better" on this benchmark is not guaranteed to be safer for real users.
5. **Thresholds are provisional**: Any cutoffs or thresholds used for visualization are provisional and should not be interpreted as validated risk categories.
6. **Not a certification tool**: The benchmark is a research instrument for comparing conditions, not a certification or compliance tool.
